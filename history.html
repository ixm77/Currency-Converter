History of Currency

Currency began as a system of bartering—trading goods and services directly. But as societies grew more complex, barter became inefficient, especially when needs didn't align. To solve this, ancient civilizations began using commodity money—objects like cattle, salt, or grain with intrinsic value. By around 3000 BCE, Mesopotamians used silver bars and the Egyptians used metal rings as early forms of money. The first standardized coins were minted in Lydia (modern-day Turkey) around 600 BCE.

As trade networks expanded, coins spread across the ancient world, followed by the invention of paper money in China during the Tang Dynasty, reaching widespread use under the Song Dynasty by the 11th century. Europe adopted paper money much later, with early banknotes issued by Swedish and Dutch banks in the 17th century. The modern era saw the rise of central banking and fiat currency—money not backed by physical commodities like gold, but by government decree. In the 20th century, most countries abandoned the gold standard entirely. The 21st century introduced digital currencies and cryptocurrencies, like Bitcoin, reflecting an ongoing evolution of what society accepts as money.
